# Bull & Bear With Me — May 01, 2026

*Apple printed money, the S&P had its best month since 2020, and the guy who built your iPhone is packing up his desk.*

THE OPEN

April's officially in the books. Best month for stocks since 2020. S&P, Nasdaq, Dow — all up big. Apple crushed earnings and popped 3% before the bell. Bitcoin nudged up. Half the globe is off today for Labour Day, so trading is quieter than a Tuesday morning at Applebee's. Good vibes. Enjoy them. Oil is at $105 and inflation is still hot, so May is already warming up in the bullpen looking unfriendly.

BIG THREE

- Apple put up $111 billion in revenue for the quarter. That's not a typo. One company. Three months. Profit was $29.6 billion, which is more than most countries' entire government budgets. Services — think the App Store, Apple TV, Apple Pay — hit an all-time high. And then they guided for 14-17% growth next quarter when analysts expected 9.5%. That's not beating expectations. That's making the analysts look like they were guessing with their eyes closed. Stock up roughly 3% premarket. Fine. Fine company. Maybe you've heard of it.

- Tim Cook is stepping down September 1st after 15 years. This was the first earnings call since that announcement dropped, and Wall Street was watching carefully. Cook's been running Apple since 2011, and in that time the stock went up something like 5,000%. The incoming CEO, John Ternus — the guy who currently runs Apple's hardware — has never run a company before. Might be great. Might be a disaster. Nobody knows yet. That's the whole game right now.

- The Fed is holding rates steady and inflation is at 3.5% using the Fed's preferred measuring stick — the PCE, which basically tracks what regular people spend money on. We've been stuck above 3% all year. The Fed's target is 2%. That gap means borrowing stays expensive. Mortgage above 6%, car loans, credit cards — none of it's getting cheaper soon. And with Powell's term expiring this month, we're about to find out what the new guy thinks about all of it.

ONE THING WORTH UNDERSTANDING

"Share buybacks" — Apple just authorized $100 billion in them, so let's actually explain what the hell that means. When a company buys back its own stock, it's reducing the number of shares floating around out there. Fewer shares means each remaining share is worth a slightly bigger piece of the pie. It's like if you and four buddies split a pizza, then one guy leaves — now you each get more. It's a way companies return money to shareholders without just handing them cash. $100 billion is a lot of pizza.

KEEP AN EYE ON

- Exxon and Chevron report this morning. Oil at $105 a barrel means energy companies are printing money right now — but it also means inflation stays stubborn.
- Powell's final weeks as Fed chair. May 15th comes fast.

THE CLOSE

Best month since 2020 is now last month's problem. May's got $105 oil, hot inflation, and a new Fed chair on the way. Good weekend though. Go outside.
