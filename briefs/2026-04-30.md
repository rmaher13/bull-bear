# Bull & Bear With Me — Apr 30, 2026

*April went out like a champ, the Fed's about to get a new boss, and Meta is spending $130 billion like it's Monopoly money.*

THE OPEN

April's done. Best month for stocks since 2020. S&P up 9%, Nasdaq up 14%, and your 401(k) statement is about to look like something you actually want to open. Take the win. Seriously. We spent a week watching oil flirt with $100 and the Fed do nothing and Big Tech stress us out with earnings — and somehow the scoreboard at the end of the month looks like this. Wild.

BIG THREE

- Meta dropped 9% after telling the world it's planning to spend somewhere between $125 and $145 billion this year — that's their "capital expenditure guidance," which is a finance way of saying "stuff we're buying." The stuff is AI. All AI. The earnings themselves were fine. Didn't matter. Wall Street looked at that number and went pale. To put it in terms that make sense: that's more than the GDP of most countries. Meta is spending the equivalent of Hungary's entire economy on AI infrastructure this year alone. Whether that's genius or insanity we won't know for five years.

- Alphabet and Amazon both beat expectations and popped. Seagate — a company that makes hard drives for AI data centers — jumped 18% because it turns out AI needs somewhere to store all that stuff it's generating. Eli Lilly crushed earnings on their weight loss drugs and jumped 7%. Starbucks beat and climbed 5%. It was a genuine mixed bag of a day — some companies got hammered, some printed money — which is exactly what the last day of an insane month should look like.

- The Fed voted to hold rates steady. We've covered this all week. What's new today is the vote was 8 to 4, which is the most dissenting votes since 1992. Four Fed officials were basically sitting there going "I don't agree with this." That's unusual. That's the Fed equivalent of a locker room argument leaking to the press. It signals that some members want rates to go up or are spooked enough about inflation that they wanted to say something on the record. And rates, by the way, aren't getting cut this year. Mortgage costs, car loans, credit cards — all staying expensive.

ONE THING WORTH UNDERSTANDING

Powell's last day as Fed chair is May 15th. Kevin Warsh takes over. Here's why this matters for you specifically: the Fed chair controls the interest rate that everything else is built on. Your savings account rate, your mortgage rate, your car payment — they all trace back to one number this person sets. When the chair changes, the thinking can change. Markets are going to spend the next few weeks reading every word Warsh has ever said, trying to figure out if he's going to cut rates or hold them or something weirder. That uncertainty moves markets. It matters. We'll be watching him closely.

THE CLOSE

April was the best month in years. Go enjoy it for about twelve hours before May shows up with a new Fed chair and a gas bill.
