# Bull & Bear With Me — Apr 15, 2026

*Banks are printing money, oil is falling, and Bitcoin just went "oh right, I do that."*

THE OPEN

Everything's up, oil's down, and for one weird Thursday in April, the market actually made sense. Enjoy it. These days don't last.

BIG THREE

- Banks had a week. JPMorgan profits up 13%. Morgan Stanley beat estimates by so much their analysts probably just lied about what the estimates were. Wells Fargo, Citi, BlackRock — all beat. When the banks are printing, the market tends to throw a party and ask questions later. That's what yesterday was.

- Oil dropped hard on Iran peace talk vibes. West Texas Intermediate — just a name for American oil, like a regional BBQ sauce — fell to around $91 a barrel after sitting above $104 earlier this month. Pakistan's army chief flew into Tehran to help broker a ceasefire extension, Trump said the war was "nearing its end," and traders immediately priced in a world where tankers can move freely again. This matters to you because gas prices and oil prices are basically the same conversation. A sustained drop here means you're not grimacing as hard at the pump by summer. Emphasis on sustained — don't go buying a truck this weekend.

- Quantum computing stocks went absolutely feral. IonQ up 21%, D-Wave up 22%, Rigetti up 13%. In one day. These are tiny companies working on computers so powerful they can theoretically solve problems regular computers can't — but they're also years away from doing anything you'd actually use. Right now they trade less like companies and more like lottery tickets. Yesterday somebody bought a lot of tickets.

ONE THING WORTH UNDERSTANDING

The Fed Chair situation is genuinely weird and worth knowing about. The Federal Reserve is the central bank — basically the entity that controls how expensive it is to borrow money in America. Your mortgage rate, your car loan, your credit card — all influenced by what those guys do. Jerome Powell has run it for years. His term ends soon. Trump nominated a guy named Kevin Warsh to replace him, and his confirmation hearing is April 21. Here's the wrinkle: one senator says he's blocking the final vote until a criminal probe into Powell gets resolved. That's not normal. Bond markets — which are basically big investors betting on future interest rates — will be watching that hearing closely. If there's uncertainty about who's in charge of the thing that controls the cost of money, markets get nervous. Fast.
