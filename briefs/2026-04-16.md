# Bull & Bear With Me — Apr 16, 2026

*S&P 500 hit 7,000 for the first time ever. Meanwhile, a strait nobody knew existed might blow up the whole party.*

THE OPEN

Records on records — S&P crossed 7,000, Nasdaq's on an 11-day win streak — and somehow the whole thing is held together with a Middle East peace deal that may or may not be real.

BIG THREE

- The S&P 500 closed above 7,000 for the first time in history and the Nasdaq hasn't had a down day in nearly two weeks. Eleven straight days of gains. That's the market equivalent of going 11-0 in fantasy and then finding out your opponent next week is healthy again. Tech led everything — Tesla ripped 7% on AI chip hype, Meta and Broadcom announced a massive chip deal, banks posted strong earnings. Genuinely good news. Enjoy it.

- The party has one very large bouncer standing outside: the Strait of Hormuz. It's a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman — about as wide as a county road relative to how much oil flows through it — and right now it's effectively shut down. Twenty percent of the world's oil and gas normally moves through there. It's been closed for over a month. Brent crude, which is basically the global price tag for a barrel of oil, already hit $127 in early April. Ken Griffin — guy who runs one of the biggest hedge funds on earth — said flat out: if that strait stays closed six to twelve months, global recession is unavoidable. That's not a Reddit comment. That's one of the most plugged-in people in finance saying the quiet part loud.

- Netflix and PepsiCo reported today. Pepsi is a signal on whether regular people are still spending — and the numbers were fine but soft. Netflix matters because it tells you whether consumers are cutting the fun stuff yet. No dramatic swings either way. Consider it a yellow light, not a red one.

ONE THING WORTH UNDERSTANDING

The Fed is stuck, and here's why that matters to your actual life. The Fed controls interest rates — think of it as the dial that makes borrowing money cheaper or more expensive. When inflation is high, they turn the dial up to slow things down. When the economy is struggling, they turn it down to get things moving. Right now, inflation is sitting at 3% and the Fed wants it at 2%. That one-point gap sounds small but it means they can't lower rates without making inflation worse. So your mortgage, your car loan, your credit card — all still expensive. And with oil spiking because of the Strait situation, groceries and gas aren't coming down either. The Fed can't save you right now. They're just watching.

KEEP AN EYE ON

- Netflix earnings — dropped after close today. Subscriber numbers will tell you a lot about whether people are still in spending mode or quietly tightening up.
- Any update on Iran ceasefire talks. Trump said it's "close to over." Markets believe him right now. If that changes, everything changes fast.

THE CLOSE

Records on Wall Street, a closed oil chokepoint, and a peace deal hanging by a thread. Normal Thursday stuff. Don't let the green numbers make you forget the Strait exists.
