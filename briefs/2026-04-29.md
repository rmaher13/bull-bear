# Bull & Bear With Me — Apr 29, 2026

*The biggest earnings night of the year, gas hit levels we haven't seen since 2022, and the Fed did exactly what we told you they'd do.*

THE OPEN

Four of the seven biggest companies on earth reported last night. The Fed announced their decision. Oil crossed $100 a barrel. Gas is $4.17 a gallon. Jerome Powell held his maybe-last-ever press conference. It was a lot. Tuesday was a warmup. Wednesday was the actual game.

BIG THREE

- The Fed held rates steady. No surprise. We walked through exactly why on Monday — inflation's still too high, oil's being a problem, every option looks bad. So they did nothing, which is sometimes the whole move. Powell's press conference was where people leaned in. He basically said: oil prices are real, inflation isn't going away fast, and we're watching. He did not cry. He did not announce his retirement plans. He just stood there being professional while everyone waited to see if he'd do something interesting. He didn't. Classic Powell.

- Microsoft, Meta, Alphabet, and Amazon all reported after the bell. Early reads are mostly strong — AI spending is still going up, not pausing, which is what the whole supply chain needed to hear. Remember last week we talked about how these four companies are so big they can move your 401(k) by themselves? Tonight is where that matters. The market will react this morning, and it could be a big swing either direction depending on what the guidance looked like on the calls. Keep your eyes open at the open.

- Gas hit $4.17 a gallon on Tuesday. That's the highest since August 2022. Oil briefly touched $100 a barrel and the reason is the same reason it's been all week — the Strait of Hormuz, that narrow water corridor in the Persian Gulf that one-fifth of the world's oil has to squeeze through, is still disrupted by the Iran conflict. Peace talks exist but nobody seems to be in a hurry. Every dollar that oil goes up, everything that moves — trucks, planes, ships — gets a little more expensive. That cost eventually lands on the shelf at the grocery store. It's a slow-moving tax that nobody voted for.

ONE THING WORTH UNDERSTANDING

You heard "the Fed held rates" and maybe you also heard "Powell's term ends May 15th." So what happens next? Kevin Warsh takes over as Fed chair. The Fed chair is basically the most powerful economic position in the country — more than most cabinet members. One person running a room of twelve sets the interest rate that ripples through every mortgage, car loan, and credit card in America. When the chair changes, the vibe can change. Markets will spend the next few weeks trying to figure out if Warsh thinks like Powell or if he's got different ideas about how fast to cut rates. The answer will move markets. We'll be watching.

THE CLOSE

Fed did nothing, Powell kept it cool, Big Tech dropped their numbers, and gas is $4.17 a gallon like it's 2022 again. A lot happened. Go get a cheap cup of coffee before that price goes up too.
